# Game Design

## Vision

Paraglide the World should make typing feel physical.

The player fantasy is simple: you are not doing drills, you are flying. Every accurate keypress becomes clean air, stable lift, and longer journeys across a living world. The keyboard stops feeling like homework and starts feeling like the control surface of a paraglider.

## Design pillars

### 1. Skill and fantasy must be the same thing

Typing cannot be a mini-game bolted onto flight. The act of using the correct finger on the correct key needs to be the literal flight mechanic.

- clean input creates lift, speed, and smoother glides
- mistakes create drag, wobble, and turbulence
- home-row return is framed as posture and recovery, not classroom instruction

### 2. Geography should feel rewarding, not decorative

Countries are not menu skins. Each destination should change:

- visual palette
- route shape and landmarks
- country radio facts and tone
- soundtrack and ambient texture
- unlockable flag glider cosmetics

### 3. Social play should help learning

Friends should make practice feel more alive.

- fly side by side
- hear each other on voice
- share route challenges
- compare clean-flight accuracy rather than only raw speed

## Core game loop

1. Pick a country or route.
2. Launch with a short typing pattern that warms up finger placement.
3. Fly through a route where words, bursts, and rhythm patterns control lift.
4. Hear country radio facts while moving through the space.
5. Finish with a score based on accuracy, consistency, and smoothness.
6. Unlock harder routes, longer flights, and social modes.

## Teaching loop

Typing instruction should be invisible but rigorous.

- Every lesson starts from home-row anchors.
- Finger hints appear before the player needs them, not after a mistake.
- Early content rewards clean posture and rhythm more than speed.
- Accuracy streaks unlock stronger glide bonuses so correct technique feels powerful.
- Adaptive lessons should identify weak keys and feed them back into future routes.

## MVP scope

The first real MVP should stay narrow:

- single-player launch to landing loop
- 3 to 5 countries with distinct lessons and radio facts
- flag-based glider identity
- adaptive typing metrics
- one route length that can be replayed quickly
- a polished results screen

The MVP should not include:

- open-world multiplayer
- live user-generated radio
- unrestricted globe traversal
- full voice moderation or matchmaking

## Phase 2 goals

After the MVP proves the learning loop:

- geospatial terrain flights
- party flights with shared waypoints
- live voice chat
- route progression by continent
- daily challenges and ghost replays

## Success metrics

The game is working if players can say all three of these:

- “I can feel my typing improving.”
- “I want to keep exploring countries.”
- “This feels like hanging out, not practicing.”
